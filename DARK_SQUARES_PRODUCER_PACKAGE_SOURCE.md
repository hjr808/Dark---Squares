# CREATOR LETTER

To whoever is reading this —

*Dark Squares* started with something I already knew.

Growing up, you learn things nobody teaches you. You learn that loyalty has a price.
You learn that ambition can look exactly like betrayal from the wrong angle. You learn
that the choices you make before you are old enough to fully understand consequence
are still yours — and that some of them follow you the rest of your life.

I watched that happen. I watched it happen to people I grew up around, to friends,
to brothers. You watch a celebration become something else in a single night, and
there is no erasing it. That night becomes the marker. Before that night and after
that night. That is what a dark square is.

Manny and Keith are not biographies. They are composites of something real — the love
between brothers, the pull of the streets, the small studio in the back room where
music felt like freedom, the birthday party that changed everything. These are
characters built from truth.

What I wanted to write — and what I believe this series can do — is show that moral
complexity is not just a dramatic device. It lives in real people, in real families,
in real neighborhoods. The tragedy of *Bloody Sweet 16* is not that something bad
happened. The tragedy is that it was inevitable, that everyone could feel it coming
and still could not stop it.

That is the show.

This is a story about what it costs to grow up. About the price of a choice made in
a moment of anger, the permanence of one wrong second, and the people who have to
carry it afterward — the brother, the mother, the friends who were there.

It is also a story about music, basketball, loyalty, and the specific light that comes
off a streetlamp on a wet night in the neighborhood where you were raised.

I believe in this project. I have been building it carefully, with discipline, because
the people it represents deserve that care. The series has twenty-one episodes of
source material, a completed narrative arc for Season One, a mapped direction for
Season Two, and a world that can sustain the kind of long-form serialized drama that
changes the conversation about urban storytelling.

I am not looking for someone to translate this. I am looking for a collaborator who
understands what it already is.

If that is you, I am ready to talk.

### The Series

*Dark Squares* is a premium one-hour serialized coming-of-age urban drama following
Manny, a young man navigating loyalty, ambition, and survival as he and his younger
brother Keith grow from childhood in the Woods into young adulthood in the Rocks —
and ultimately toward the night that changes everything.

Season One spans 21 episodes, covering Manny's journey from the rules of the
Woods — a neighborhood that teaches children how to read danger, silence, and
respect before they understand why — through the discovery of music and basketball
as pathways to identity, through the formation of a close circle, and to a single
birthday party that transforms from celebration into the irreversible event the
neighborhood calls **Bloody Sweet 16**.

This is not a show about crime. It is a show about consequence. It is about the cost
of a single moment of violence in the rain and what everyone who loved each other
is left holding when the sirens arrive.

---

### Why This Story, Why Now

Urban television drama has expanded dramatically, but the specific emotional texture
of *Dark Squares* — the intimacy of the brother relationship, the role of music as
both creative sanctuary and social danger, the moral weight placed on a sixteen-year-old
boy who did not choose to grow up this fast — remains underserved.

*Dark Squares* is not a genre exercise. It is character-driven, consequence-centered,
and built on the kind of specificity that only comes from authentic knowledge of the
world it depicts. Every location, every street language pattern, every nursery rhyme
in the stairwell, and every record played at Coney Island is exact.

The series is designed for the premium long-form serialized drama audience. It rewards
patient, close attention. It has the emotional scope of *The Wire* and the family
intimacy of *Euphoria* while occupying its own distinct moral universe.

---

### Creative Foundation

**Season One** is sourced from a completed 21-episode Book One narrative, spanning:

- **The Woods** (Episodes 1–6): Childhood, neighborhood rules, the first music lessons,
  a family governed by Ella Mae's strict boundary between home and street.
- **The Rocks** (Episodes 7–13): The move to a new neighborhood, earned respect,
  the formation of the full circle — Manny, Keith, Butch, Lucky, Nate, Sarah.
- **The Invitation** (Episodes 14–21): High school, romantic connections, the stakes
  of small street-money activity, and the inevitability of the Sweet Sixteen.

**Season Two** begins in the final seconds of Episode 21 and covers three years of
aftermath — the legal, emotional, and relational consequences of Bloody Sweet 16,
including Keith's developing music career (as Li Keith), Sarah's harrowing Season Two
arc, Butch's escalating legal pressure, and Manny's impossible position at the center
of it all.

# SERIES SYNOPSIS

## Dark Squares — Season One

---

### Overview

*Dark Squares* is the story of two brothers, a neighborhood, and the permanent cost
of a single night.

Manny is four years older than Keith. He is careful, observant, and carries a
protective weight that no one asked him to carry but everyone expects him to hold.
Keith is charismatic, funny, and magnetic — the kind of person crowds open up for,
which is exactly why Manny watches him so closely. Their mother, Ella Mae, works late
shifts and governs the house with one overriding rule: **do not bring drama or the
streets to this house.** They live in a four-family building in the Woods, where the
neighborhood teaches children to grow up fast and vigilant.

Season One follows the brothers across twenty-one episodes, from their childhood in
the Woods to the night the neighborhood calls Bloody Sweet 16.

---

### Act One — The Woods (Episodes 1–6)

Manny and Keith grow up in the Woods, a neighborhood that doubles as classroom and
test. They navigate alleys, empty lots, a junkyard they treat as a kingdom, and a
graveyard that becomes the site of a childhood dare. Kizzy, their dog, moves with
them through all of it. Ella Mae holds the household together with discipline and love,
but her late shifts give the boys borrowed time outside — time they use carefully,
and sometimes don't.

Music enters early. A friend named Steve owns turntables. Manny watches, then learns.
He discovers that he can feel the time, the space between records, the place where
one sound hands off to another. Keith discovers he can freestyle — naturally, easily,
in a way that stops people in place.

Episode 5, **Steve's Turntables**, marks the point at which music stops being
background and becomes a possible future.

---

### Act Two — The Rocks (Episodes 7–13)

Ella Mae moves the family to the Rocks. New house. Basement, garage, yard, fence,
porch. Room that belongs to them. The Woods taught Manny to read danger; the Rocks
teaches him to play basketball and use angles.

Newcomers are tested. Manny and Keith earn their place by refusing to be easy prey
without trying to rule anything. In the garage, Manny finds a court. On the court,
he finds Butch — dark-skinned, patient, sharp-eyed, unimpressed by almost everything,
and immediately impressive to Manny.

Steve connects Manny to Lucky's room: a makeshift studio in the back of a house where
Lucky teaches by working, fixes broken equipment, and keeps a gun for protection because
music draws everyone — including people who want what you've built. Keith walks through
the door and, one afternoon, freestyles over one of Manny's tapes. Lucky recognizes it
immediately. Keith has something.

The circle begins to form. Manny and Butch develop a quiet partnership — shared respect,
shared small risk, shared responsibility. Coney Island enters the picture in Episode 13
(**Coney Nights**): a late-night gathering place where the group spends limited money,
shoots dice nearby, and Nate joins them — a witness who becomes a member.

---

### Act Three — The Invitation (Episodes 14–21)

High school expands the world. Sarah joins — she has light brown hair and green eyes,
a calm posture and a soft but firm manner. She meets Butch first at the lockers. She
learns the group gradually: Manny, Keith, Lucky, Nate. She does not judge the Woods or
perform amazement at the Rocks. She listens and understands what the studio means to
its creators.

Episode 18, **The Chocolate Cake**, is quiet and specific: Sarah makes Manny a
homemade chocolate cake for his sixteenth birthday. It is a private gesture. It creates
a connection between them that remains unconsummated but real.

Manny plans his Sweet Sixteen at Ella Mae's house. Keith invites Chucky — a friend from
the Woods, someone from his earlier world. Ella Mae leaves Uncle Candyman in charge and
goes to work.

Episodes 19–21 (**The Backyard**, **Last Dance**, **Bloody Sweet 16**) cover the party
in real time.

Lucky DJs. Cheerio takes the microphone first. Then Keith freestyles, and the backyard
stops. Sarah gives Manny his cake. Butch helps keep things calm. Manny plays funk for
the final dance.

Chucky arrives feeling invited. He leaves feeling ignored. The party runs long; Keith
is pulled toward family, music, and the role of the night. Chucky sits at the edges.
Sarah introduces him to Michelle, and for an hour he has a reason to stay. But after
the last song, the attention is already somewhere else.

Chucky confronts Keith. The argument moves outside. Rain falls.

In the rain, in front of Ella Mae's house, Keith and Chucky fight. Keith lifts Chucky
and slams him onto the concrete. He says immediately that he did not mean to.
Chucky does not respond.

Uncle Candyman calls 911. The birthday banner hangs crooked. Lucky's last record
scratches to silence.

The celebration becomes **Bloody Sweet 16** — the event the neighborhood will
remember forever.

---

### The Cost

Season One ends with Chucky unresponsive and the consequences beginning. Manny cannot
protect Keith from what has already happened. Ella Mae will come home to a house that
is not the same house she left. The circle — Manny, Keith, Butch, Lucky, Nate, Sarah —
will have to carry the night together.

What the night costs is what Season Two is about.

# SERIES CONCEPT AND THE MEANING OF DARK SQUARES

## What Is a Dark Square?

The title is not metaphorical decoration. It is the series' central moral architecture.

A **dark square** is a consequence that has permanence. Every choice creates a shadow.
Most shadows fade. Some don't. Some choices leave a permanent moral marker — a weight
you carry forever, visible to others if they know where to look. Those are dark squares.

The concept is not supernatural. There are no rules posted anywhere in the story. It is
discovered through lived experience. Characters learn what a dark square is by acquiring
one — by doing something they cannot undo, or by watching someone they love do it.

The series is built around the accumulation of dark squares: small ones, large ones,
and finally the one that defines the story.

**Bloody Sweet 16** is the central dark square of Season One. It is not the only one.
By the time the birthday party becomes that name in the neighborhood's memory, we have
watched Manny acquire smaller shadows through his partnership with Butch, watched Keith
accumulate the casual consequences of someone who moves through crowds without looking
back at who he has left behind, watched Lucky keep a gun for protection because the
studio draws everyone — including what you are protecting yourself from.

Every choice has been building toward the rain outside Ella Mae's house. Every dark
square that came before has made the final one possible.

---

## The Visual Grammar of the Concept

The series uses visual darkness to reinforce the concept without literalizing it.
The palette — deep black, metallic silver, midnight blue, gun-metal gray — is not
about grimness. It is about weight. Light exists in this world: the light off a
streetlamp on wet pavement, the light from a television in the dark, the light in
a basement filled with birthday noise. The contrast between that light and the darkness
around it is the visual statement of the series.

Gates and entrances are recurring images: thresholds between neighborhoods, between
childhood and something else, between the world you know and what happens next.
Silhouettes. Shadows. The specific quality of moonlight over an urban street.

The camera does not make the neighborhood exotic. It makes it specific. There is a
difference between those two things, and that difference is the show.

---

## The Concept in Practice

In Episode 1, **The Woods**, Manny describes his neighborhood as a place that teaches
children to read faces before they can read a room. The woods are where dark squares
are first planted — before anyone knows that is what they are.

By Episode 21, **Bloody Sweet 16**, the lesson has been paid in full.

Season Two begins in the final seconds of Episode 21 and carries the question that
every character in the series must eventually answer: *What do you do with a dark square
you cannot erase?*

# TONE, THEMES, FORMAT, AUDIENCE, AND FRANCHISE VISION

## Tone

*Dark Squares* occupies a specific emotional register: dark urban realism with gothic
sensibilities. The tone is consequential and street-level authentic. It is not
sentimentalized and not exploitative. It does not aestheticize poverty or use the
neighborhood as backdrop for generic crime plotting.

The show is moody, specific, and human. Danger is present throughout, but it is never
the most important thing in any scene. The most important thing is always what the
danger costs — to Manny's relationship with Keith, to Ella Mae's rule about keeping
the streets outside, to Lucky's ability to make music in the same room where he keeps
a gun for protection.

The balance between danger and warmth — between the threat of the street and the
love of the brothers, the humor of the circle, the specific pleasure of a record
played at exactly the right moment — is the tonal challenge the series is built
around. It is sustained across twenty-one episodes of source material.

**Tone references:** *The Wire* (moral weight and system-level consequence) ·
*Euphoria* (stylized emotion and intimate youth perspective) ·
*The Chi* (Chicago neighborhood specificity) ·
*When They See Us* (institutional consequence of a single event)

*Dark Squares* is not a remake of any of these. It occupies its own tonal space.

---

## Themes

### Primary Themes

**1. Loyalty versus ambition.**
Every major relationship in the series tests the boundary between these two drives.
Manny is loyal to Keith and to Ella Mae's rule, but his ambition — in music, in his
small street partnership with Butch — pushes him toward risk. Keith is loyal to Manny
but follows crowds. Butch is loyal to Manny but understands that loyalty has a
practical limit. The tension is not resolved. It escalates.

**2. The permanence of consequence — dark squares.**
The series' central moral concept (see Series Concept section). Choices leave shadows.
Some shadows are permanent. The show is about watching people learn this the hard way.

**3. Brotherhood and the cost of protection.**
Manny's protective relationship with Keith is the emotional spine of the series.
Protection is not control — the show learns this the hard way. Manny cannot follow
Keith everywhere. He cannot make Keith's choices. The night Bloody Sweet 16 becomes
the neighborhood's name for what happened is the night Manny discovers what
protection actually costs.

**4. Music as identity, craft, and danger.**
Music in *Dark Squares* is not background. It is narrative action. Steve's turntables
are the first time Manny believes a future is possible outside the Woods. Lucky's
studio is a place where the group can be who they might become — and also a place
where a gun lives because talent draws danger. Keith's freestyle is his voice, his
ambition, his identity. The music thread is woven into every arc in Season One.

**5. Survival and the moral code.**
Every character in *Dark Squares* has developed a practical moral code for survival.
Ella Mae's code: the house is protected ground. Manny's code: protect Keith, honor
the house, earn what you take. Butch's code: understand the network, stay patient,
choose your moments. Lucky's code: protect the music. The series is about what
happens when those codes collide with a night that none of them planned for.

**6. Urban struggle, resilience, and generational cycles.**
The Woods and the Rocks are not just settings. They represent the specific social
grammar of neighborhoods that have been underserved, overpoliced, and misrepresented.
The generational dimension — what was planted before Manny and Keith were born, what
they will pass forward — is present in the background of every scene.

## Audience

**Primary audience:** Adult viewers of premium serialized drama, ages 18–45.
Specifically: audiences who engage with character-driven urban drama on platforms
such as HBO, Showtime, Netflix, Amazon, FX, Hulu, and similar premium or prestige
streaming/cable outlets.

**Secondary audience:** Urban music and culture enthusiasts who value authentic
representation; coming-of-age drama audiences; viewers of true-crime and
consequence-driven narrative.

**Platform fit:** Premium cable (HBO, Showtime, FX) or major streaming service
(Netflix, Amazon Prime Video, Apple TV+, Hulu). The series' 21-episode Season One
structure and its serialized architecture are a natural fit for binge-release or
weekly serialized programming strategy.

## Franchise Vision

*Dark Squares* is designed as a multi-platform, multi-season franchise.

**Television:** Season Two is story-mapped. Season Two covers the three years
following Bloody Sweet 16 — aftermath, consequences, and the difficult paths of
every character who was present. Later seasons are directionally planned.

**Literary:** Season One exists as a companion prose narrative (Book One) — a
21-section manuscript that follows the same arc in literary form. A Book Two is
planned for Season Two. The literary series creates a separate audience and a
franchise asset that extends the series' commercial reach.

**Music:** The series is deeply rooted in hip-hop and music production culture.
Keith's eventual artist identity (Li Keith) and the group's relationship with music
as craft create natural opportunities for a legitimate music component — soundtrack,
original music, artist development — tied to the series' narrative.

**Merchandise:** The Dark Squares brand — visual identity, taglines, character
iconography — has been developed to production quality and is ready for commercial
application. The visual palette, logo system, and character visual hierarchy are
fully established.

**Longevity:** The series' thematic architecture — the accumulation of dark squares
across seasons and across characters — is designed for the kind of long-form
consequence-driven storytelling that sustains audience loyalty across multiple seasons.
Season One ends with a tragedy that creates questions. The answers span multiple seasons.
The franchise earns its own mythology.

# SEASON ONE OVERVIEW

## Dark Squares — Season One

**Episodes 1–21**
**Narrative Arc: From the Woods to Bloody Sweet 16**

---

### Season One at a Glance

Season One is the origin story — not of a hero, but of a consequence.

Over twenty-one episodes, we follow Manny and Keith from childhood in the Woods to
the night that changes everything. The season is organized around three phases of
the brothers' lives: growing up in the Woods, building a new identity in the Rocks,
and the party that brings every character they have met, every choice they have made,
and every dark square they have accumulated into one night.

**The promise Season One makes:** This show keeps every dark square on the board.
Nothing is forgotten. Every choice builds. The birthday party at the end of Episode 21
is not a surprise. The tragedy is that it was always coming — and that everyone who
loved each other could feel it and still could not stop it.

---

### Phase One: The Woods (Episodes 1–6)

The season opens with Manny's narration over the Woods — a neighborhood that teaches
children to grow up before they are ready. Ella Mae governs the household with a single
overriding rule: the streets stay outside. The apartment is in a four-family building,
near the basement hallway and back entrance. Their dog Kizzy accompanies the brothers
through this period.

The Woods is where Manny and Keith learn to read faces, respect streetlights as curfew,
and understand that a small conflict can follow you home. It is also where childhood
happens: the junkyard as their kingdom, the graveyard bet at night, Steve's turntables
as the first real dream of a future.

Key establishing episodes:
- **Episode 1, The Woods** — World is introduced; Manny's voice is established.
- **Episode 3, Junkyard Kings** — The brothers' childhood bond and the specific
  geography of their imagination.
- **Episode 5, Steve's Turntables** — Music enters as a possible future.
- **Episode 6, Night Lights** — The tension between the brothers' world expanding
  and Ella Mae's rule holding them in place.

---

### Phase Two: The Rocks (Episodes 7–13)

Ella Mae moves the family to the Rocks. New house. Own door. The Rocks represents
upward mobility, but it comes with a test: newcomers are not welcomed automatically.
Manny and Keith earn their place through basketball, restraint, and the refusal to
be easy prey without starting a war.

This phase introduces the complete circle:
- **Butch** (Episode 9) — Met in the Rocks driveway; basketball establishes mutual
  respect; friendship grows into partnership.
- **Lucky's Room** (Episode 10) — Steve connects Manny to Lucky; Lucky's makeshift
  studio becomes the group's creative center; Keith's freestyle is discovered.
- **Nate** (Episode 13, Coney Nights) — Nate joins the group at Coney Island;
  a witness who will become essential.

Key story developments:
- Manny and Butch begin small street-money activity — shared profit, shared risk.
- Keith's freestyle talent establishes his voice and his social magnetism.
- Lucky's studio holds both artistic ambition and the gun he keeps there for protection.
- Coney Island at night — a gathering place where the group's bond deepens.

---

### Phase Three: The Invitation (Episodes 14–21)

High school expands the world. Sarah (Episode 15) enters through Butch at the lockers.
She joins the circle gradually, without performing amazement at what she finds.

Manny's sixteenth birthday is planned. Keith invites Chucky — a Woods friend, someone
from an earlier world. The invitation is generous. What happens with it is not.

Episode 18, **The Chocolate Cake**, marks the quietest moment in the season: Sarah
makes Manny a homemade chocolate cake. It is a private gesture between two people
who understand each other. No scene in Season One is louder than this one is quiet.

Episodes 19–21 are the party. The basement holds food, gifts, noise. The backyard holds
the music. Lucky DJs. Cheerio takes the mic first, then Keith — and when Keith
freestyles, the backyard stops. After the last dance, Chucky — who came feeling invited
and leaves feeling ignored — confronts Keith outside in the rain.

The fight. The slam. The silence.

Uncle Candyman calls 911.

**Bloody Sweet 16.**

---

### Season One Closing Image

The birthday banner hangs crooked. Lucky's last record scratches to silence. Manny
cannot reach Keith through what has already happened. The celebration is over.

What begins next is Season Two.

---

### Season One Tone Summary

Season One is deliberately paced. It earns the tragedy through twenty episodes of
investment in the characters, the neighborhood, and the circle. By the time the party
ends in rain and silence, the audience has spent months with these people. The tragedy
lands because it was always there, embedded in every small choice along the way.

No episode is wasted. Every title in the episode guide belongs to this story and to
no other.

# SEASON ONE EPISODE GUIDE

## Dark Squares — Episodes 1–21

**Season One contains exactly 21 episodes. No episodes beyond Episode 21 are
part of Season One.**

---

### Episode 1 — The Woods

**Theme:** World-building and introduction of Manny's perspective.

The season opens with the Woods — the neighborhood where Manny and Keith grew up.
Manny's narration establishes the rules of this place: how to read a face, when to
cross the street, how a small conflict can follow you home. We meet Ella Mae, whose
overriding rule governs the household. We meet Kizzy, the brothers' dog, who moves
with them everywhere.

*Key elements:* The four-family building. The basement hallway. The back entrance.
Ella Mae's late shift. Kizzy's bark as an alarm system. Manny's protective voice
established.

---

### Episode 2 — Monday

**Theme:** The rhythm of ordinary life and the specific pressure of the Woods.

The brothers navigate a school week inside the Woods. The neighborhood is not
constantly dangerous — it is constantly vigilant. The difference matters. Monday
establishes the routine that the rest of the season will disrupt.

*Key elements:* Street rules. Curfew. Watching who comes to the door. The specific
social pressure of being known in a neighborhood that knows everything.

---

### Episode 3 — Junkyard Kings

**Theme:** Childhood imagination and the specific geography of the brothers' bond.

Manny and Keith treat the junkyard as their kingdom. They build futures there in their
heads — imaginary empires made from rusted metal and neighborhood loyalty. Kizzy
accompanies them. The episode is the warmest in the season and the most vulnerable.

*Key elements:* The junkyard. The brothers as childhood allies before they become
something more complicated. Kizzy as part of the crew.

---

### Episode 4 — The Graveyard Bet

**Theme:** Courage, dares, and the specific intimacy of a shared risk.

The brothers take a nighttime dare to the graveyard. A shared childhood memory that
will surface later in the season as a reference point — something that belonged to
them before everything got complicated.

*Key elements:* The graveyard. Night. The dare. The specific pleasure of a fear shared
between brothers.

---

### Episode 5 — Steve's Turntables

**Theme:** Music as possibility — the first sight of a future.

Steve has turntables. Manny watches him work and hears something in the way records
connect — the place where one sound hands off to another. It is the first time music
feels like something Manny could do, could own. Steve is Manny's doorway into the craft.

*Key elements:* Steve's room. The turntables. Manny learning to listen. The first
sense of music as identity rather than entertainment.

---

### Episode 6 — Night Lights

**Theme:** The tension between the world expanding and the rules that hold it in place.

Ella Mae's late shifts give the brothers borrowed time outside. Streetlights are the
curfew. The episode tracks the specific freedom and the specific danger of the hours
after the light goes on — the neighborhood at night, and what the night asks of
people who are too young to answer it safely.

*Key elements:* Streetlights as curfew. The night as both freedom and risk. Manny's
protective instinct beginning to complicate the pleasure of being outside.

---

### Episode 7 — The Move to the Rocks

**Theme:** Progress and the price of a new world.

Ella Mae moves the family to the Rocks. New house. Own front door. Basement, garage,
yard, fence, porch. The physical upgrade is real — but the Rocks has its own tests.
Manny and Keith arrive as outsiders in a neighborhood with its own rules.

*Key elements:* The new house. Ella Mae's satisfaction at the move. The first encounter
with the Rocks' social hierarchy.

---

### Episode 8 — The Test

**Theme:** Establishing presence without provocation.

Newcomers to the Rocks are tested through questions, physical play, and comments
designed to find weakness. Manny and Keith earn the message that they are not easy
prey without trying to rule anything. The test takes different forms for each brother.

*Key elements:* Neighborhood tests. The difference between the Woods and the Rocks
as social environments. Kizzy. The garage as the beginning of a basketball court.

---

### Episode 9 — Butch

**Theme:** The beginning of Manny's most important friendship.

Butch is visiting family in the Rocks when he and Manny meet in the driveway with a
basketball. He is dark-skinned, calm, patient, sharp-eyed, and not interested in
impressing anyone. Their friendship begins with mutual respect earned through play.
Butch loves blues, soul, rock, guitar. He says he sings rather than raps. He
understands money, connections, and loyalty as a network.

*Key elements:* The driveway basketball game. Butch's stillness. The first sense that
this friendship will matter. The beginning of the Manny/Butch bond.

---

### Episode 10 — Lucky's Room

**Theme:** The studio as creative home — and the gun that lives there.

Steve introduces Manny to Lucky. Lucky's makeshift room — equipment rigged and repaired,
levels balanced, records organized — becomes the group's creative center. Lucky teaches
by working. He fixes broken gear. He listens for what is wrong in a sound before he
fixes it. He also keeps a gun for protection, because talent draws everyone who wants
what you've built.

*Key elements:* Lucky's room/studio. Lucky's teaching style. The gun. Keith's first
freestyle over Manny's beats — and Lucky recognizing immediately what Keith has.

---

### Episode 11 — Dope Fein Dinner

**Theme:** Street economics and the specific comedy of survival.

Old Man Willie trades a grill for something he needs. The "dope fein dinner" scene —
a moment of bleak humor and neighborhood resourcefulness — introduces the way street
economics work at the most basic level. The episode balances comedy and consequence.

*Key elements:* Old Man Willie. The grill trade. Street economy. The specific humor
that comes from understanding exactly what is happening.

---

### Episode 12 — The Max Julian

**Theme:** The cost of looking like you belong.

Manny acquires a Max Julian coat — the kind of coat that makes a statement in the
neighborhood about who you are and how seriously you take yourself. Two older boys
rob him of it at a bus stop in winter. The theft is not just a loss. It is a lesson
about visibility and vulnerability.

*Key elements:* The Max Julian coat. The bus stop. The robbery. Manny's response —
not rage, but a careful recalibration of how he moves.

---

### Episode 13 — Coney Nights

**Theme:** The circle completes — and the night feels like the beginning of something.

Manny, Butch, Lucky, and Keith gather at Coney Island — a late-night spot where the
group spends limited money, shoots dice nearby, and exists in the specific warmth of
being together after dark. Nate is here tonight. He walks with them to Lucky's studio.
He is a witness who is now a member.

*Key elements:* Coney Island. The group together. Dice nearby. Nate's arrival into the
circle. The sense that something is being built.

---

### Episode 14 — First Day

**Theme:** The world of high school and its new social grammar.

High school. A new environment with different rules, different hierarchies, and the
locker hallways where social worlds intersect. The episode introduces the space in
which Sarah will appear in Episode 15.

*Key elements:* High school setting. The expanded social world. The particular
visibility and anonymity of a new environment.

---

### Episode 15 — Sarah

**Theme:** A new person who joins the circle on her own terms.

Sarah has light brown hair, green eyes, a calm posture, and a soft but firm manner.
She meets Butch first at the lockers. She learns the group gradually: Manny, Keith,
Lucky, Nate. She does not judge the Woods or perform amazement at the Rocks. She
listens. She recognizes what the studio means to the people who built it. She becomes
part of the circle without announcing herself.

*Key elements:* Sarah's introduction. Her meeting with Butch. Her first conversations
with Manny. The quiet accuracy of how she sees the group.

---

### Episode 16 — The Fallen

**Theme:** The weight of what has accumulated — and the first sense that something
cannot be held in place much longer.

The circle is fully formed. The street-money partnership between Manny and Butch has
been operating. Lucky's studio has been producing. Keith is developing his voice.
The episode registers the shadow all of this casts — the "fallen" refers to the sense
that the circle has climbed high enough that the fall, when it comes, will matter.

*Key elements:* The accumulation of risk. The growing stakes. The first real
foreshadowing of the Sweet Sixteen's outcome.

---

### Episode 17 — The Invitation

**Theme:** The party is planned. Every invitation matters.

Manny plans his Sweet Sixteen at Ella Mae's house. Keith invites Chucky — a friend
from the Woods, someone from an earlier world. Ella Mae prepares. Uncle Candyman is
designated the adult in charge. The logistics of the party are also a map of the
relationships that will collide.

*Key elements:* The planning of the party. Keith's invitation to Chucky. Ella Mae's
preparations. Uncle Candyman's role. The guest list as a portrait of everyone Manny
has accumulated.

---

### Episode 18 — The Chocolate Cake

**Theme:** The quietest episode in the season — a private gesture between two people.

Sarah makes Manny a homemade chocolate cake for his sixteenth birthday. It is a private
gesture. Manny and Sarah are alone with it for a moment. It creates a connection between
them that is real and unconsummated — something that belongs specifically to these two
people. No scene in Season One is louder than this one is quiet.

*Key elements:* The homemade cake. Sarah and Manny's private connection. The specific
warmth before the storm of Episodes 19–21.

---

### Episode 19 — The Backyard

**Theme:** The party is alive — and so is everything that could go wrong.

The party begins. The basement holds food, gifts, and the warm noise of a family
celebration. The backyard holds the music. Lucky DJs. Cheerio takes the microphone
first. Then Keith freestyles — and the backyard stops. The energy is real, joyful,
and at its peak.

*Key elements:* The basement and backyard party. Lucky's DJ set. Cheerio with the mic.
Keith's freestyle electrifying the crowd. The party at its best, before it turns.

---

### Episode 20 — Last Dance

**Theme:** The long arc of the night's final hours, and the fault lines beginning to show.

The party outlasts its planned ending. Manny plays funk for the final dance. Sarah
gives Manny his cake. Manny and Sarah clean the basement; Butch joins them. Chucky
feels marginalized. Sarah introduces him to Michelle. For an hour, Chucky has a reason
to stay — he dances with Michelle, promises to take her shopping. The episode is a
sustained piece of narrative tension: everything good happening at once, and everything
that is about to go wrong already in motion.

*Key elements:* The after-party. The final dance. Manny and Sarah cleaning. Butch's
help. Chucky's growing isolation. Michelle. Nutty Nate's Check Check messages tracking
the rising tension. Sarah's attempt to include Chucky.

---

### Episode 21 — Bloody Sweet 16

**Theme:** The cost arrives. The dark square is placed.

After the last song, Chucky confronts Keith about feeling left out. The argument moves
outside. Rain falls. In front of Ella Mae's house, in the rain, Keith and Chucky fight.
Keith lifts Chucky and slams him onto the concrete. He says immediately that he did not
mean to. Chucky does not respond.

Uncle Candyman calls 911. The birthday banner hangs crooked. Lucky's last record
scratches to silence.

The celebration becomes **Bloody Sweet 16** — the event the neighborhood will
remember forever. Manny cannot protect Keith from what has already happened.
Ella Mae is still at work. The sirens are coming.

*Key elements:* The confrontation. The rain. The fight. The slam. Keith's immediate
realization. Chucky unresponsive. Uncle Candyman calling 911. The banner. The silence.
The name that will attach to the night.

**Season One ends.**

# PRINCIPAL CHARACTER PROFILES

## Dark Squares — Season One

---

### MANNY
**Role:** Protagonist | Keith's older brother | Son of Ella Mae

Manny is four years older than Keith. He carries a protective weight that no one
formally assigned him but everyone expects him to hold. When Ella Mae works late,
Manny is the responsible one. He has internalized that role so completely that he
cannot separate it from who he is.

He is observant, guarded, and disciplined. Alone, he is introspective — he thinks
before he speaks and feels things longer than he lets on. In public, he reads
situations before he enters them, reads faces before he reads words, and waits for
the moment that tells him whether a situation is safe or not.

Basketball earns him respect in the Rocks. He plays with rhythm and quickness, using
angles and space rather than size. The game is a form of thinking for him.

Music is his craft. Steve introduces him to turntables. Lucky deepens his knowledge
of equipment, levels, records, timing, and production. Manny does not show off his
musical ambition — he develops it quietly, the same way he develops everything.

He recognizes Keith's freestyle talent early and takes pride in it with the specific
pride of an older brother who sees a younger sibling surpass him in something
they both love.

His partnership with Butch — small street-money activity, shared profit and shared
risk — is real. He does not pretend it is not what it is, but he also does not let
it consume him. He keeps Ella Mae's trust as the central organizing value of his life.

He plans his Sweet Sixteen as a celebration in Ella Mae's house. He keeps it clean.
He values the house not as property but as the boundary between the life they are
building and the street.

Season One ends with Manny unable to protect Keith from the consequence of Keith's
fight with Chucky. He was standing close. He still could not stop it.

**Spelling note:** The detailed narrative source uses "Manny." Planning documents
also use the alternate spelling "Mannie." The canonical spelling for detailed-narrative
material is Manny.

---

### KEITH
**Role:** Manny's younger brother | Natural freestyler | Social magnet

Keith is charismatic, funny, and easy to love — which makes him easy to follow into
trouble. He does not have Manny's discipline or Manny's instinct to wait before moving.
He seeks the crowd, makes people laugh, and thrives in the center of a room.

Manny believes this is why Keith attracts both friends and problems. Keith's social
magnetism is not a flaw — it is his nature. But nature and safety are not the same
thing in the neighborhoods they grew up in.

Keith discovers his voice by freestyling over one of Manny's practice tapes. He
does not practice it — it is just there, complete, fully formed. When Lucky hears it,
he recognizes it immediately. Keith becomes a central voice in Lucky's studio.

At Manny's Sweet Sixteen, Keith freestyles in the backyard and electrifies the crowd.
He is the reason the night has its best moment. He is also the reason it ends the way it does.

He invites Chucky to the party because Chucky is his friend. He becomes occupied with
the role of the night — his own moment, the people around him, the music — and does
not notice that Chucky is sitting at the edges. He is not careless about Chucky on
purpose. The party just takes over.

After the last song, in the rain, when Chucky confronts him, Keith does not back down.
The fight happens fast. He lifts Chucky and slams him onto the concrete. He says
immediately that he did not mean to. He is telling the truth.

Season One ends with Keith looking at what he cannot undo.

---

### ELLA MAE
**Role:** Manny and Keith's mother | Working parent | Guardian of the home

Ella Mae works late shifts. Her absences give the brothers borrowed time outside —
time she is aware of and accepts because she trusts Manny, up to a point.

Her overriding household rule is absolute: do not bring drama or the streets to this
house. The house is protected ground. It is the thing she has worked for. The move to
the Rocks is the material evidence of that work — a house with a basement, a yard, a
porch, a door that belongs to the family.

She trusts Manny with the Sweet Sixteen while she works. She leaves Uncle Candyman
in charge. She believes in Manny's judgment. Season One ends with her still at work,
unaware of what has happened to the house she left him to protect.

---

### BUTCH
**Role:** Manny's closest peer and partner | Keith's friend | Romantic partner to Sarah

Butch is from the Woods but visits family in the Rocks. He and Manny meet in the
driveway with a basketball. Their friendship begins with mutual respect and grows
from there.

He is dark-skinned, calm, patient, and sharp-eyed. He is not interested in impressing
anyone. He observes carefully and speaks precisely. He loves guitar, blues, rock,
soul, and rap — he hears sadness inside a chord and says he sings rather than raps.

He understands money, connections, opportunity, and loyalty as a network. His
familiarity with street activity exceeds Manny's, but he is not reckless with it.
He and Manny become partners in small street-money activity. He helps keep the party
safe — takes an ill guest home, manages the crowd.

Sarah meets him first at the high-school lockers. Their closeness develops naturally.
Manny respects the relationship. There is no established love triangle or betrayal
between Manny, Butch, and Sarah.

---

### LUCKY
**Role:** Studio host | DJ | Producer | Member of the core circle

Steve introduces Manny to Lucky. Lucky's makeshift room becomes the group's creative
center. He teaches by working: fixing equipment, listening for sound problems, setting
levels, teaching timing. He takes the music seriously as a craft and holds the space
for everyone else to take it seriously too.

Lucky keeps a gun in the studio because music draws friends, strangers, talent, envy,
and danger. He is not paranoid — he is practical. The danger is real, and he has
decided that the studio is worth protecting.

He recognizes Keith's freestyle talent immediately. He DJs Manny's Sweet Sixteen,
plays the final record of the night, and is present in the late-party sequence when
everything changes.

# WORLD AND LOCATION OVERVIEW

## Dark Squares — Setting and Geography

---

### The World of Dark Squares

*Dark Squares* takes place in an unspecified contemporary American city. No specific
city name, state, or exact calendar year is established. The setting is recognizable
as inner-city urban America — the specific kind of neighborhood that has been
underserved, tested, and defined by its own rules. The camera treats it with specificity
rather than generality.

The action begins in the Woods and moves to the Rocks.

---

### The Woods

The Woods is where Manny and Keith grow up. It is the first neighborhood — their
childhood classroom, their first social test, and the place that teaches them
everything they will need to survive the move to the Rocks.

**Physical description:**
- A four-family building, low, near the basement hallway and back entrance.
- Alleys, empty lots, a junkyard, a graveyard, porches, courts, and street corners.
- The specific quality of a neighborhood that is always awake and always watching.

**Social rules:**
- Ella Mae's apartment is protected ground — drama stays outside.
- Streetlights signal curfew; Ella Mae's late shifts give borrowed time after dark.
- Respect, reputation, and loyalty have material consequences.
- You learn to read faces before you read a room.

**Key locations within the Woods:**

| Location | Significance |
|---|---|
| Ella Mae's apartment | The brothers' early home; protected and governed by Ella Mae's rule. |
| The junkyard | The brothers' childhood kingdom; where futures are imagined. |
| The graveyard | Site of the nighttime dare; a shared childhood memory. |
| Steve's room | Manny's entry point to turntables and beat-mixing. |
| Alleys and street corners | The social world of the neighborhood. |

---

### The Rocks

The Rocks is the second neighborhood — where Ella Mae moves the family for more space
and the material sense of progress. It offers more than the Woods, but requires its
own test before newcomers belong.

**Physical description:**
- Ella Mae's house in the Rocks: basement, garage, yard, fence, porch, and a front
  door belonging to the family.
- The garage becomes a basketball court.
- The basement holds birthdays, food, and noise.
- The porch is a lookout; the fence is a boundary.

**Social rules:**
- Newcomers are tested through questions, comments, and physical play.
- Earning respect requires refusing to be easy prey without starting a war.
- The Rocks has its own hierarchy, different from the Woods but not less serious.

**Key locations within the Rocks:**

| Location | Significance |
|---|---|
| Ella Mae's house in the Rocks | The family's second home; site of the Sweet Sixteen and Bloody Sweet 16. |
| The garage court | Where Manny plays basketball; where he and Butch first meet. |
| The basement | Party space; celebration; the warm center of family life. |
| The backyard | Where Lucky DJs the Sweet Sixteen; where the music lives. |
| The front of the house | Where the fight happens; where Chucky is unresponsive; where Uncle Candyman calls 911. |

---

### Recurring Locations Across Neighborhoods

| Location | Role |
|---|---|
| Lucky's room/studio | Makeshift creative center; where Keith's freestyle is discovered; where a gun lives for protection. |
| Coney Island | Late-night gathering spot; limited money, dice nearby; where Nate joins the circle (Episode 13). |
| The high school | Where Sarah meets Butch and gradually enters the circle. |
| The bus stop | Where Manny is robbed of his Max Julian coat; Nate is first seen. |

---

### The Studio (Lucky's Room)

Lucky's room deserves its own entry because it is the emotional and creative center
of the second half of Season One. It is makeshift — equipment rigged and repaired,
not purchased new. It is precise — Lucky listens for problems in sound before he
fixes them. It is alive — music is made here, talent is recognized here, the group
is most itself here.

And a gun lives in it. Because music draws everyone. Friends, strangers, talent,
envy, and the people who want what you have built. Lucky is practical, not paranoid.
The studio is worth protecting.

---

### Coney Island

Coney Island is established canon as a late-night gathering place for the group
(Episode 13, *Coney Nights*). The group spends limited money, shoots dice nearby,
and it is here that Nate is fully incorporated into the circle. The Coney Island
setting represents the specific pleasure of the group's world — a little money, a
late night, freedom that is not quite free.

---

### Visual Geography

The visual palette of *Dark Squares* treats these locations as specific, lived-in
places rather than generic urban backgrounds.

- **Colors:** Deep black, metallic silver, midnight blue, gun-metal gray.
- **Lighting:** Practical sources — streetlamps, moonlight, kitchen light, neon.
  High contrast. Selective highlights. Rim lighting on character faces.
- **Composition:** Urban architecture as framing. Gates and entrances as thresholds.
  Silhouettes. Street-level perspective. The specific detail of a real neighborhood.
- **Atmosphere:** Moody, specific, and human. Darkness supports emotional weight;
  it does not replace narrative clarity.

# TIMELINE AND CONTINUITY OVERVIEW

## Dark Squares — Series Timeline

---

### How Time Works in Dark Squares

*Dark Squares* does not use a fixed calendar date or a named city. The story takes
place in a contemporary American inner-city. Time moves through the brothers' lives —
through childhood, adolescence, and the specific night that marks the boundary between
before and after.

The fundamental timeline is biographical: Manny is four years older than Keith.
Manny turns sixteen at the Sweet Sixteen party in Episode 21. These are the anchor
points of Season One.

# FUTURE-SEASON DIRECTION

## Dark Squares — Season Two and Beyond

---

### Notice

This section provides directional planning for future seasons to demonstrate the
franchise's long-form potential. Specific episode titles, exact story outcomes,
character resolutions, and later-series developments beyond what is noted here
are not established canon and are not disclosed in this document.

---

### Season Two — Direction

**Position:** Season Two begins in the final seconds of Season One Episode 21.
The rain, the sirens, the unresponsive Chucky, and the circle standing in the
aftermath of a night they cannot undo.

**Scope:** Season Two covers approximately three years following Bloody Sweet 16 —
the immediate aftermath, the first full year under scrutiny, the second year as the
circle begins to rebuild, and the third year as consequences and ambitions reach a
threshold point.

**Central question:** What do you do with a dark square you cannot erase?

---

### Season Two Story Threads

**Manny's arc:** Manny manages family, home, music, and a series of impossible moral
choices. He must learn that protection cannot become control. His authority over the
circle — earned in Season One — creates a burden in Season Two. He cannot lead everyone
through this the same way.

**Keith's arc:** Keith lives under scrutiny. He cannot use his voice publicly in the
immediate aftermath of Bloody Sweet 16. His music development is suppressed by the
weight of what happened. His eventual emergence as an artist — under the name
**Li Keith** — is a Season Two milestone. Li Keith is Keith's artist name; he is not
a separate character. His development remains local throughout Season Two.

**Butch's arc:** Butch becomes a practical leader with followers he never asked for.
His familiarity with the street world creates escalating legal pressure. No specific
charge, verdict, sentence, or jurisdiction is established for this season.
Butch and Sarah's relationship continues.

**Nutty Nate's arc:** Nate becomes an essential organizer, witness, and voice of
judgment for the circle. His reliability and observation are what distinguish him.
His Check Check presence in Season One becomes consequential in Season Two — the
thread is evidence.

**Sarah's arc:** Sarah faces a serious personal crisis in Season Two — a major
permanent turning point. She is a survivor with agency; her arc is not defined by
other characters' response to what happens to her.

**Pretty Ricky:** A local music mentor who enters the group's world in Season Two.
He expands the circle's access to craft, discipline, and performance preparation.
He was not present at Bloody Sweet 16. He does not replace Lucky's original studio
role.

---

### Season Two Guardrails

The following story elements are expressly not introduced until later seasons,
if at all:
- Any New York relocation or New York-named connection.
- Any character named Wizard or reference to the Wizard door.
- Any invitation/secret-society storyline.
- Later-series death structures or large-scale outcome resolutions.

These guardrails preserve the serialized integrity of the franchise and prevent
Season Two from resolving questions that belong to later seasons.

---

### Beyond Season Two

*Dark Squares* is designed for a multi-season run. The franchise's thematic
architecture — the accumulation of dark squares across characters and generations —
is built for long-form consequence-driven storytelling.

The literary companion series (novels) will expand the world in parallel with
the television seasons. Book One maps to Season One. Book Two maps to Season Two.

The music component of the series — rooted in hip-hop, turntable culture, and
Keith's eventual artist identity as Li Keith — creates a legitimate music franchise
dimension that extends the commercial scope of the project.

# PILOT SAMPLE SECTION

## Dark Squares — Episode 1: "The Woods"

### Pilot Episode Overview

The pilot introduces Manny's world through his own perspective — not as a narrator
looking back, but as someone who is living inside it and beginning to understand what
it means.

The episode establishes the three rules of the Woods:
1. Read the room before you enter it.
2. Know who is watching before you move.
3. What happens outside stays outside.

The pilot is the foundation of everything that follows. Every element introduced here
— the neighborhood, Ella Mae's rule, Kizzy, the specific geography of childhood
in the Woods — will bear weight by Episode 21.

---

### Scene Sample — Opening Narration and World Establishment

*The following is a representative sample section drawn from the Season One source
material. Full pilot screenplay available upon request.*

---

**EXT. THE WOODS — NEIGHBORHOOD STREET — EARLY EVENING**

The neighborhood settles into the hour before streetlights. Not quiet. Not loud.
The specific frequency of a block that is always paying attention.

A four-family building. Low and wide. The kind of building that has been here
longer than any of its current residents.

A DOG — small, tan, watchful — sits on the front step. This is KIZZY. She is
facing the street.

A BOY, fourteen, comes around the corner. This is MANNY. He is not rushing. He is
reading. His eyes move over the block the way someone reads a room they have been
in a hundred times: not looking for anything new, but confirming that nothing old
has changed.

He checks the building's front door.
He checks the driveway.
He checks the corner where the older boys sometimes stand.

Corner is clear.

He relaxes — slightly, not completely. Relaxing completely is not something you do
in the Woods.

Kizzy gets up and moves to him.

---

**MANNY (V.O.)**

*The Woods taught me something school never did. It taught me how to read a face
before the face decided what it was going to be. Taught me how to cross the street
before a situation became a situation. Taught me that a small thing — a wrong look,
a borrowed dollar, a girl someone decided was spoken for — could follow you to your
front door if you weren't paying attention.*

*Most people call it a neighborhood. We called it the test. The test never stopped.*

---

Manny reaches the front step. He sits next to Kizzy. She leans against him.
He watches the street.

From inside the building — from the apartment low in the building, near the basement
hallway — the sound of a television.

---

**INT. ELLA MAE'S APARTMENT — CONTINUOUS**

Small. Specific. Everything in it earned.

ELLA MAE, late thirties, moves through the kitchen with the efficiency of a person
who has a shift to get to and a house to hold together before she leaves.

KEITH, ten years old, sits at the kitchen table with his homework in front of him and
his eyes on something else entirely.

**ELLA MAE**

Keith. That homework.

**KEITH**

I'm doing it.

**ELLA MAE**

You're looking at the wall.

**KEITH**

I do both.

Ella Mae gives him a look. The look does not require words. Keith looks at the homework.

**ELLA MAE**

Manny's home?

**KEITH**

He's on the steps.

She nods. She moves to the window. She can see the front step from here — just barely,
at an angle. Manny and Kizzy, watching the street.

Something in her face: relief she will not say out loud.

---

**INT. ELLA MAE'S APARTMENT — DOORWAY — MOMENTS LATER**

Ella Mae stands in the doorway in her work uniform.

**ELLA MAE**

The house rule.

This is not a question.

**MANNY**

The house rule. Yes.

**ELLA MAE**

What is it?

**MANNY**

Whatever's out there stays out there.

**ELLA MAE**

*Whatever's out there stays out there.* And?

**MANNY**

And if you bring it in, it's on you.

She holds his look for a moment.

**ELLA MAE**

Don't be out late. Streetlights come on, you're in.

**MANNY**

I know.

**ELLA MAE**

I know you know. Say it anyway.

**MANNY**

Streetlights come on, we're in.

She pulls the door behind her. The lock clicks.

Manny sits with Kizzy.

The street settles into the hour.

---

### Production Note

The pilot establishes Manny's voice — observant, protective, quietly ambitious —
through environment before dialogue. The neighborhood is not explained; it is
demonstrated. Ella Mae's rule is stated once and owned completely by the end of
the scene.

All production elements in the pilot — the neighborhood geography, character voices,
dialogue rhythm, and Ella Mae's house rule — are sourced from the Season One narrative
manuscript and represent established canon.

### Visual Design Principles

**Photography and Composition:**
- Framing: architectural elements — gates, doorways, urban geometry — as compositional
  borders.
- Depth: multiple layers of silhouettes and environmental context.
- Perspective: street-level, intimate camera angles.
- Symmetry: centered composition with environmental balance.

**Lighting:**
- High contrast (3:1 to 5:1 ratio minimum).
- Practical sources: streetlamps, neon, moonlight, window light.
- Strong rim lighting on character faces for definition.
- Minimal fill — dramatic shadows are preserved.

**Color Grading:**
- Desaturated overall with intentional accent colors.
- Crushed blacks; slightly lifted midtones.
- Cool temperature with warm accent from practical sources.
- Subtle vignetting to focus visual attention.

---

### Typography

**Primary font:** Helvetica
- Main titles: Helvetica Bold, all caps, 72pt+, letter-spaced +3 to +5 points
- Taglines: Helvetica Bold, 18–24pt, Title Case
- Cast names: Helvetica Regular, 14–16pt
- Body/credits: Helvetica Regular, 10–12pt

---

### Imagery Guidelines

**Elements to include:**
- Urban architecture: building facades, streets, industrial spaces
- Silhouettes: characters backlit against environmental elements
- Gates and entrances: symbolic of boundaries and transitions
- Moonlight: ethereal, ominous, contemplative
- Group dynamics: character positioning showing relationships
- Street culture: authentic clothing, basketball, music equipment

**Elements to avoid:**
- Bright, saturated colors (outside story-specific sequences)
- Computer-generated effects or artificial-looking imagery
- Cheerful or uplifting imagery
- Clean, corporate aesthetics
- Clichéd urban drama tropes
