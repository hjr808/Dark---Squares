# DARK SQUARES

## Location Lookbook

### Created by Alfred HJR

**Scope:** Text-only production design guidance for confirmed Book One / Season One environments. This document does not make an unverified place, business, hospital, police setting, or future-world detail canonical.

---

## Series Visual and Environmental Philosophy

Locations are lived emotional systems, not generic urban backdrops. The Woods holds childhood, vigilance, play, and memory. The Rocks offers physical progress—space, a yard, a garage, a basement, a front door—but not immunity from consequence. The camera and design must show contrast without treating either neighborhood as uniformly poor, dangerous, wealthy, or safe.

The production period is contemporary but intentionally not pinned to a year, city, street name, school name, or address. Avoid modernizing design by inference and avoid an artificial period overlay. Exterior and interior contrast matters: the street can press against Ella Mae’s apartment door, while the house in the Rocks creates new rooms, boundaries, and party geography. Day holds social texture and childhood play; night concentrates attention, music, weather, and risk.

Use naturalistic practical light, restrained deep black/metallic silver/midnight blue/gun-metal-gray guidance, and specific sound rather than generalized decay. Let porches, fences, hallways, courts, records, appliances, traffic, voices, rain, and silence tell the story.

---

## Location Continuity Rules

- The Woods and the Rocks are distinct neighborhoods; the move occurs in Episode 7.
- The “Dark Squares” concept is a metaphor for consequence, not a physical Square neighborhood.
- Ella Mae’s Woods apartment is low in a four-family building near the basement hall, laundry room, and back entrance.
- Ella Mae’s Rocks house has a porch, garage, basement, yard, fence, and front area. Its party geography is fixed: basement = food/gifts/cake/cleanup; backyard = DJ/music/dancing; behind garage = Candyman/basketball-team beat; front = rain, fight, 911, banner/balloon aftermath.
- Lucky’s confirmed space is **Lucky’s room / studio**. It is not established as a basement studio.
- Do not assign street names, addresses, building keys, across-the-hall relatives, city, school name, hero vehicles, landmark businesses, gang markings, police presence, or new neighborhood boundaries.
- The Episode 21 confrontation moves from the wet backyard to the **front of Ella Mae’s house**. Do not relocate the fight to the backyard.

## ELLA MAE’S WOODS APARTMENT

### Story Function
The early family refuge and the place where Ella Mae’s rule keeps drama and the streets outside. It establishes Manny’s responsibility for Keith.

### Confirmed Geography
Low apartment in a four-family building, near the basement hall, laundry room, and back entrance; thin walls carry building noise.

### Economic and Social Identity
Modest, cared for, active, and pressured by proximity—not a shorthand for neglect.

### Exterior / Interior Design
Keep the building entrance, apartment door, hallway, sink, floor space, work bag, and dinner-dish reality repeatable. Interior practicals, windows, laundry/basement circulation, and the threshold must remain legible.

### Color, Lighting, Camera, and Sound
Muted household warmth, thin daylight, and practical lamp/kitchen light. Controlled mediums and close threshold framing support the home rule. Footsteps, hallway activity, door knocks, dishes, laundry/building hum, and Kizzy’s presence carry texture.

### Character Associations / Season One Events
Manny, Keith, Ella Mae, and Kizzy. Episodes 1–2 establish the rule and the door conflict; Episodes 5 and 7 continue the family/music/move progression.

### Props, Continuity, and Production Considerations
Bat in Episode 2 remains defensive and does not become inside-the-home violence; turntable practice tape appears in Episode 5. Preserve the apartment door/hallway relationship and Ella Mae’s work-bag entrance. Controlled interior and hallway coverage should be planned together.

## THE WOODS

### Story Function
Manny and Keith’s childhood classroom, warning, playground, and test; also Butch’s origin environment.

### Confirmed Geography
Includes streets, alleys, empty lots, porches, courts, street corners, a junkyard, and graveyard. Its exact city limits and boundaries are unestablished.

### Design, Lighting, Camera, and Sound
Faded brick, asphalt, washed color, porches, fences, bikes, and layered human activity without stereotyped danger dressing. Day supports games and movement; dusk/night brings streetlights, distance, and vigilance. Use observational wides, moving group frames, traffic, voices, bikes, dogs, basketball, and late-night quiet.

### Character Associations / Events
Manny, Keith, Ella Mae, Kizzy, early Chucky social-world reference, and Butch’s origin. Episodes 1–6 root childhood here; Episode 7 frames departure without erasure.

### Continuity / Production
Do not portray it as uniformly poor or dangerous. Different sublocations must remain distinguishable for children, night play, junkyard, and graveyard scenes.

## THE ROCKS AND ELLA MAE’S NEW HOUSE

### Story Function
The Rocks represent material progress and a new social test. Ella Mae’s house becomes the family’s new center and the Sweet Sixteen’s dramatic container.

### Confirmed Geography
Cleaner streets, bigger houses, driveways, measuring porches, and social scrutiny. Ella Mae’s house includes porch, garage, basement, yard, fence, and front area.

### Exterior / Interior / Sound
More open driveways and usable yard depth, not luxury. Show porch observation and fence boundaries. Inside, establish basement access and party circulation before the party arc. Day carries basketball/social testing; night carries music and crowd sound.

### Character Associations / Events
Manny, Keith, Ella Mae, Butch, Sarah, Lucky, Nate, Chucky, Candyman, Cheerio, and Michelle. Episodes 7–21 use this world; the garage/court starts basketball respect and the house carries the party/finale.

### Continuity / Production
No address, vehicle ownership, adjacent-house plan, or floor plan is established. Treat the garage, basement, backyard, fence, porch, and front driveway as repeatable story geography.

## BASEMENT, GARAGE / COURT, BACKYARD, AND FRONT OF HOUSE

### Story Function
These are one house’s distinct dramatic zones, not interchangeable party coverage.

### Confirmed Design and Events
The **basement** holds food, gifts, Sarah’s chocolate cake, cleanup, and the Manny/Sarah/Butch non-jealous sequence. The **garage/court** supports basketball and party logistics. The **backyard** holds Lucky’s DJ table, speakers, records, dancing, freestyle, alcohol, and final-song energy. The **front of house** holds the rain-front fight, record scratch aftermath, 911 call, crooked banner, loose balloon, and red streaks.

### Props / Lighting / Sound
Basement: tables, gifts, cake box, food, practical warmth. Backyard: DJ table, speakers, cords, milk crates, records, fence, music bleed. Front: wet pavement/ground, rain, banner, balloon, depleted music, voices, and siren-free endpoint—do not add police or hospital action.

### Continuity / Production
The confrontation begins after wet-backyard tension but occurs at the front; preserve movement and weather. Plan crowd control, rain effects, music playback, cable safety, minors, and stunt choreography. No damage/injury aftermath beyond the established tableau.

## LUCKY’S ROOM / STUDIO

### Story Function
Creative second home where Manny learns production, Keith’s voice becomes undeniable, and dreams coexist with danger.

### Confirmed Geography and Design
It is a room/makeshift studio, not confirmed as a basement. Equipment includes turntables, mixer, levels, crates, records, cables, and a gun kept for protection. Keep a workable performance/listening axis and believable equipment access without inventing a house plan.

### Lighting, Camera, Sound, and Events
Midnight blue/gun-metal restraint, warm practical pools, indicator light, vinyl black, close tactile inserts, and careful voice/beat playback. Episodes 10, 13, and 15 establish instruction, group entry, Sarah’s recognition, and Keith’s freestyle.

### Continuity / Production
The gun is not a finale weapon or casual display. Music equipment must remain functionally consistent. Controlled playback and clearance planning are required.

## Location Relationship Map

The Apartment sits within the Woods childhood world; the Woods carries memory and social history after the move. ## Standing Set Recommendations

Prioritize Ella Mae’s Woods apartment/hallway, Ella Mae’s Rocks house (basement, garage/court, backyard, porch/front), Lucky’s room/studio, a flexible Woods street/alley, a Rocks driveway/court, and high-school hall/locker coverage.
