## SLIDE 2 — The Hook

Two brothers grow from the Woods into the Rocks, where music, basketball, and hard-won friendship offer a way forward—until a Sweet Sixteen celebration becomes the consequence none of them can undo.

## SLIDE 3 — Series Logline

Protective older brother Manny tries to turn music and basketball into a future for himself and his younger brother Keith, but every promise, invitation, and survival choice leaves a shadow that reshapes their circle.

## SLIDE 4 — Why This Story Matters

- A coming-of-age story where ordinary details—home rules, records, courts, cake, and rain—carry lasting consequence.
- Family responsibility, belonging, ambition, and survival are emotionally universal stakes.
- Music is lived culture and dramatic action, not background decoration.
- The story holds humor, warmth, talent, threat, and moral complexity in the same frame.

## SLIDE 6 — The World

- **The Woods:** Childhood classroom, warning, playground, and test.
- **The Rocks:** Material progress, new social tests, and Ella Mae’s house.
- **High school:** The circle expands; Sarah meets Butch first.
- **Lucky’s room/studio:** Creative center where dreams and danger coexist.
- **Hills / Menjo:** Not established in controlling Season One production material; require Alfred HJR approval.
- **Dark Squares:** Symbolic consequence, never a physical neighborhood.

## SLIDE 7 — The Meaning of Dark Squares

Every choice casts a shadow. Some choices leave permanent moral markers: dark squares. The concept is experiential, not supernatural. Season One’s celebration becoming **Bloody Sweet 16** is the defining example.

## SLIDE 8 — Main Characters

**Manny:** Observant older brother, basketball player, and developing DJ/producer whose discipline cannot protect Keith from every consequence.
**Keith:** Charismatic younger brother and natural freestyler, pulled toward connection, laughter, and the crowd.
**Butch:** Manny’s basketball rival turned chosen brother, musician, small-money partner, and Sarah’s established romantic connection.
**Sarah:** Perceptive high-school friend who meets Butch first, recognizes the crew’s talent, and brings steadiness to the circle.

## SLIDE 9 — Supporting Ensemble

- **Ella Mae:** Working mother and guardian of the home boundary.
- **Uncle Candyman:** Trusted chaperone, comic street philosopher, and imperfect adult authority.
- **Lucky:** Studio host, technician, producer, and party DJ.
- **Cheerio:** Party mic presence before Keith’s freestyle.
- **Nate / Nutty Nate:** Quiet witness turned group member and Check Check observer.
- **Pretty Ricky / Boy George:** No completed production profiles; require Alfred HJR approval before presentation as characters.

## SLIDE 10 — Season One Journey

The Woods establishes Manny, Keith, Ella Mae, Kizzy, childhood memory, and music’s first spark. The move to the Rocks creates space and new tests; basketball brings Butch, the studio deepens craft, Coney brings Nate, and high school brings Sarah. Manny’s Sweet Sixteen gathers the world at Ella Mae’s house. Music, belonging, exclusion, rain, and consequence transform the night into Bloody Sweet 16. The Season One endpoint remains unresolved beyond Chucky being unresponsive.

## SLIDE 11 — Signature Storytelling Style

- Narrator framing turns memory, place, innocence, and consequence into emotional context.
- “Old MacDonald” appears only at the Episode 21 opening; no repeating rhyme format is established.
- Nate’s two pre-confrontation Check Check messages are the only established Chat sequence.
- Turntables, tape, freestyle, DJ choices, funk, and the final record scratch function as plot action.
- Endings land on emotional cost, not manufactured resolution.

## SLIDE 12 — Themes

Loyalty versus ambition. Survival versus a personal moral code. Family responsibility. Friendship and belonging. Music as craft and possibility. Money, access, attention, and consequence. Loss of innocence.

## SLIDE 13 — Visual Identity

Grounded urban realism with gothic sensibilities: deep black, metallic silver, midnight blue, and gun-metal gray. The Woods has childhood texture and vigilance; the Rocks has physical progress without innocence. Practical light, clear geography, tactile music equipment, and emotionally legible night work keep the series authored rather than stereotyped.
