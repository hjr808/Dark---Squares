# DARK SQUARES

## Visual Lookbook

### Created by Alfred HJR

**Purpose:** A production-ready visual-direction document for the completed Book One / Season One record. This lookbook describes execution, not new story. It introduces no new character, location, vehicle, costume fact, music cue, or future-season event.

---

## Visual Identity

*Dark Squares* is grounded urban realism rendered with cinematic intention. The camera stays close to how characters read a room, a street, a court, a record, or a face—not to sensationalize their world. Childhood holds motion, play, invention, and open space; consequence narrows the frame, deepens the shadow, and makes thresholds feel heavier.

Performances should be lived-in and unforced. Humor is not a release from the drama; it is part of how this community survives. Music, basketball, family warmth, danger, and silence must be allowed to coexist in the same visual language. The image should feel authored and atmospheric, never glossy, exploitative, or stereotyped.

**Core visual contrast:** The Woods and early childhood have room for imagination. The Rocks offer material progress and more space, but not freedom from the past. By the Sweet Sixteen, a familiar family house becomes charged by music, bodies, weather, and the cost of attention.

---

## Color Palette

The franchise palette is anchored in **deep black, metallic silver, midnight blue, and gun-metal gray**. Color should track emotional state and time of day rather than label characters as good or bad.

| Story world | Palette and progression |
|---|---|
| Apartment years | Worn warm practicals, muted brown/amber household tones, aged paint, soft daylight through windows. The refuge is modest but cared for. |
| The Woods | Faded brick, asphalt gray, washed denim, dirty greens, sodium-vapor amber, and winter blue. Daylight allows childhood texture; dusk brings vigilance. |
| The Rocks | Cleaner daylight, restrained neutral siding, driveway concrete, porch light, grass and fence texture. It should feel more spacious, not idealized. |
| Schools | Institutional neutrals, fluorescent whites/greens, lockers, hall sheen, and small personal color accents that support social expansion. |
| Lucky’s Studio | Midnight blue, gun-metal gray, red/amber equipment indicators, vinyl black, cable shadows, and warm lamp pools. Creative energy exists beside unease. |
| Sweet Sixteen | A controlled evolution from warm party color and food-table light into saturated backyard music color, wet blue-black rain, and the diminished aftermath at the front of the house. |
| Night and music scenes | Preserve faces inside darkness with selective highlights from lamps, porch lights, speaker LEDs, streetlights, and DJ equipment. Darkness must never hide essential action. |

As Season One darkens, reduce visual ease rather than simply desaturating the picture: deepen negative space, let warm sources become isolated, and make the final rain and crooked banner carry the changed emotional temperature.

## Cinematography

### Camera language

- **Childhood / Woods:** Observational wides for games, bikes, alleys, junkyard imagination, and the brothers moving together; handheld movement can carry play and restless freedom.
- **Family rules and intimate conversations:** Use controlled medium shots and close-ups that respect silence, listening, and the protected interior of Ella Mae’s home.
- **Basketball:** Let wide geography establish the court and social test; use low, kinetic detail for feet, hands, ball, rims, driveways, and Manny’s use of angles and space.
- **Music:** Move between tactile inserts—needle, vinyl, fader, tape, microphone, hands—and expressive close-ups that make performance an event.
- **Conflict:** Handheld work should be motivated by rising instability, not used as a default. Keep geography legible, particularly at the party and front-of-house finale.

### Framing and movement

Use wide shots to establish neighborhood systems: the four-family building, porch, court, backyard, basement, fence, and front yard. Use close-ups sparingly but decisively for Manny’s observation, Keith’s voice, Ella Mae’s rules, Butch’s restraint, Sarah’s listening, Nate’s phone, and the physical evidence left after the final confrontation.

Tracking shots are useful when the group moves through a shared world—street, school hall, Coney approach, or party flow. Locked frames suit home rules, studio concentration, and moments when a character has nowhere to go. Slow motion is not an established formal device; reserve it for an Alfred HJR-approved editorial choice and never use it to glamorize violence. Montage may compress established music practice, neighborhood play, or the party’s evolving energy while retaining chronology and emotional cause-and-effect.

## Lighting

| Environment | Lighting direction |
|---|---|
| Homes | Practical lamps, kitchen light, window daylight, porch light, and modest household warmth. A home is a boundary; it should not look like a generic set. |
| Streets | Motivated streetlights, storefront spill, headlights only where a vehicle is actually established, moonlight, and reflected wet pavement. |
| Lucky’s Studio | Layered practicals from equipment, lamps, and limited window/door spill. Protect skin tone and musical detail inside the dark palette. |
| School | Credible institutional fluorescent light softened by natural window light and hallway reflections. |
| Party | Basement food/gift warmth; backyard DJ light and speaker-driven energy; behind-garage shadows; front-of-house rain and harder practical sources. |
| Emotional scenes | Let proximity to a practical source reveal inner state: kitchen warmth, studio lamp, porch light, or the colder isolation of a street at night. |

## Production Design

### Homes and neighborhood

The Woods apartment is a low four-family-building refuge with a hall, laundry/back-entry context, evidence of family routines, and the pressure of the street just beyond the door. Ella Mae’s Rocks house is production-critical: porch, garage court, basement, backyard, fence, and front area all carry story function. Design those spaces for repeatable geography and credible movement before the Sweet Sixteen is filmed.

The Woods needs alleys, empty lots, porches, courts, a junkyard, and a graveyard; each should read as a real childhood map rather than an abstract “danger” environment. The Rocks needs driveways, larger houses, watching porches, and visible social space. High school should feature lockers, hallways, lunch/social zones, and basketball-practice context without inventing a school name.

### Studio, court, and party objects

Lucky’s room needs turntables, mixer, records, crates, cords, speakers, practice-media texture, and a believable working layout. The protection gun is a controlled prop in the studio context only; it is not the finale weapon and must not be visually foreshadowed as one.

Basketball courts and driveways must support real play. The party design must preserve its established geography: main floor mostly off-limits except bathroom; basement for food, gifts, cake, and cleanup; backyard for DJ/music; behind garage for the basketball-team/Candyman beat; front of house for the fight and aftermath.

### Vehicles, jewelry, and unestablished locations

No recurring vehicle, vehicle ownership, jewelry system, or Menjo location is established. Do not assign a hero car, branded jewelry, route, school identity, city, or new neighborhood landmark without creator approval.

## Costume Direction

Wardrobe should be street-authentic, practical, character-specific, and consistent with the show’s restrained palette. Avoid caricature, costume shorthand, conspicuous labels that lack clearance, or styling that predicts unestablished future status.

| Character | Direction from established character function |
|---|---|
| Manny | Functional, observant, athletic/creative presentation that supports basketball, music work, and family responsibility. Do not lock an unsupported signature look. |
| Keith | Youthful, expressive, mobile, and socially connective; allow performance moments to feel earned rather than costumed. |
| Butch | Calm, unshowy, practical, and musically individual. Avoid using wardrobe to overstate his money knowledge or future position. |
| Sarah | Calm, practical, and self-possessed; do not sexualize her or use costume to create a Manny–Sarah triangle. |
| Ella Mae | Working-parent practicality, home authority, and care; wardrobe should reflect lived routine rather than a fixed profession not established on screen. |
| Uncle Candyman | Trusted adult presence with comic warmth and neighborhood authority; avoid costume parody. |
| Lucky | Working studio practicality: movement, technical access, and an environment full of equipment. |
| Chucky | Party-era clothing should support his wish to belong without visually prejudging his outcome. |
| Neighborhood ensemble | Distinct silhouettes and authentic variation; no invented affiliation markers, uniforms, or hierarchy. |

**Naming boundary:** The completed production record uses **Manny** and **Keith**. “Mannie” is planning-only wording, and “Li Keith” is not an established canonical name.

## Hair and Makeup

Maintain continuity through the established Woods-to-Rocks and school/party chronology rather than inventing dated eras or a three-year transition. Early childhood should retain ordinary, active, outdoor wear-and-tear; school and studio material can show social maturity without prematurely aging characters. The Sweet Sixteen requires a clear continuity map for party arrival, heat/dancing, alcohol context, rain, damp hair/clothing, and the final front-of-house tableau.

Makeup should preserve natural texture. Fatigue, weather, and emotional stress should be specific to the scene, not generalized “grit.” No injury, medical, or post-finale makeup state may be designed as canon because Chucky’s outcome is unresolved.

---

## Music Style

The sound identity is rooted in turntables, records, practice tapes, hip-hop, freestyle, funk, and Butch’s stated love of guitar, blues, rock, soul, and rap. Use original score and licensed tracks to support place, memory, rhythm, and emotional transition; the production record does not lock specific songs, artists, score composer, or licensing plan.

Silence is active storytelling. It can isolate an emotional realization, turn a room after a performance, or expose the weight of the record scratch. **Gospel influence is not an established recurring canon element**; do not build it into the sonic identity without Alfred HJR-approved source authority.

---

## Editing Style

- **Drama:** Favor reaction, eye lines, and clear cause-and-effect over explanatory coverage.
- **Comedy:** Let timing come from performance, pauses, and the group’s shared rhythm; do not cut away from the humor too quickly.
- **Music scenes:** Cut through hands, equipment, performer, listener, and changing room energy so music advances the scene.
- **Arguments:** Preserve spatial clarity and the emotional escalation; avoid music-video treatment of conflict.
- **Emotional endings:** Land on the episode’s memory, warning, consequence, or unresolved fact.
- **Cliffhangers:** Episode 21 ends with established visual/sound evidence—the scratched record, 911 call, rain, crooked banner, and unresponsive Chucky—not a fabricated resolution.

---

## Opening Titles

Use source-aligned visual elements: deep black, metallic silver, midnight blue, gates, moonlight, city architecture, records, courts, porches, fences, hands, and thresholds. Movement should feel deliberate and consequential, not generic crime-drama imagery. A final title sequence, theme song, and title-card treatment require Alfred HJR approval.

## Episode Endings

There is no mandatory visual formula beyond the completed production record. Endings should preserve emotional cost and the episode’s specific last image: a remembered childhood moment, a warning, a relationship shift, a music discovery, or an unresolved fact. Do not impose a repeating cliffhanger or narrator device on future material without source authority.

## Production References

Aim for premium television production values: tactile locations, restrained color control, clear geography, naturalistic performance, intentional sound, and emotionally legible night work. Reference points may be used internally for craft discussion by department heads, but *Dark Squares* must not copy another production’s imagery, scene construction, soundscape, character styling, or narrative identity.

## Director Notes

The audience should feel the beauty, humor, and possibility that make the Woods and the Rocks worth loving before it feels the cost of the choices made there. The series should invite viewers to inhabit Manny and Keith’s world closely enough that a court, a record, a birthday cake, a porch, or a final rainstorm carries emotional history. Hold on the human reaction. Let music create connection. Let consequence remain honest.
