## 1. Series Overview

### Premise — CONFIRMED CANON

*Dark Squares* is a character-driven urban coming-of-age drama about Manny, his younger brother Keith, and the circle they build as they grow from the Woods into the Rocks. Music, basketball, family responsibility, loyalty, and survival give them possible ways forward; each choice also carries a consequence—a “dark square.” Book One / Season One culminates in Manny’s Sweet Sixteen, when a celebration is transformed into the unresolved event called **Bloody Sweet 16**.

### Genre

**CONFIRMED CANON:** Urban coming-of-age drama with family drama, music drama, street-level realism, and an ensemble friendship story. The concept is experiential and moral, not supernatural.

### Themes — CONFIRMED CANON

- Loyalty versus ambition.
- Survival versus a personal moral code.
- Family responsibility and the weight Manny carries for Keith.
- Music as craft, voice, community, and possibility.
- Friendship, belonging, money, status, and attention.
- Childhood memory, loss of innocence, and the visible cost of choices.
- Consequence as lived experience rather than a simple lesson.

### Tone

**CONFIRMED CANON:** Grounded, emotionally direct, street-level, and character-driven. Humor, family warmth, music, threat, and consequence coexist. Characters are not simplified into heroes or villains, and urban life is not treated as caricature.

### Target audience and rating

**FUTURE POSSIBILITY / REQUIRES ALFRED HJR APPROVAL:** No completed production source fixes a target demographic, platform, formal content rating, or episode-length rating. The material’s mature consequence, street language, substance context, and violence should be assessed by the creator, producing partners, and distributor during packaging.

### Why this series is unique — production positioning, not new canon

The completed record combines intimate brotherhood with music as plot action: a practice tape can reveal a voice, a DJ decision can alter a room, and a scratched record can mark the end of childhood. The Woods and the Rocks are not generic backdrops; they are social worlds with different tests, memories, and rules. The series makes its central idea—each decision leaving a shadow—visible through relationships rather than exposition alone.

## 4. World of *Dark Squares*

### The Apartment and the Woods — CONFIRMED CANON

Manny and Keith grow up in Ella Mae’s low four-family apartment building in the Woods, near a basement hallway, laundry area, and back entrance. The Woods is their classroom, warning, playground, and test. It contains alleys, empty lots, porches, courts, street corners, a junkyard, and a graveyard. Ella Mae’s house rule—do not bring drama or the streets inside—makes the apartment a protected refuge, not an escape from reality.

### The Rocks and Ella Mae’s house — CONFIRMED CANON

Ella Mae moves the family to a house in the Rocks. The neighborhood offers cleaner streets, larger homes, driveways, a garage, porch, basement, yard, fence, and a front door that belongs to the family. It is material progress, not automatic safety or innocence. The garage supports basketball; the basement and backyard become the Sweet Sixteen’s central geography; the front of the house becomes the finale’s site.

### School — CONFIRMED CANON

High school expands the social world in Episode 14, introducing Sarah at the lockers and creating a setting for hallways, lunches, jokes, basketball practice, and a larger circle. No school name, grade, city, or calendar year is fixed.

### Lucky’s Studio — CONFIRMED CANON

Lucky’s room is a makeshift studio and a second creative home. Equipment, records, crates, levels, mixing, listening, and performance live beside a gun kept for protection. The studio embodies the show’s central tension: dreams and danger share the same space.

### Streets, homes, and community rules — CONFIRMED CANON

Respect, reputation, curfew, money, loyalty, and inclusion have material consequences. The Woods teaches vigilance; the Rocks tests newcomers through attention and physical play. Family homes have boundaries, most explicitly Ella Mae’s rule that street conflict stays outside.

### Coney Island, transit, and other recurring story spaces — CONFIRMED CANON

Coney Island is a late-night restaurant/parking-lot social hub and the setting for Nate’s entry into the core group. A winter bus stop and city bus are the setting for Manny’s Max Julian coat robbery and Nate’s first direct contact with him. Old Man Willie’s grill/trap-house setting is an Episode 11 moral-comedy location rather than an established recurring base.

### Menjo — REQUIRES ALFRED HJR APPROVAL

No completed production source establishes Menjo as a location, business, home, or recurring world element. It must not be presented as current canon.

### Social hierarchy — CONFIRMED CANON

The show’s social order is experiential rather than formally diagrammed: basketball earns respect, music creates belonging, money changes access, adults set imperfect boundaries, and attention can include or exclude. Do not convert this into an invented gang, criminal, school, or class hierarchy.

## 5. Character Profiles

### Manny — CONFIRMED CANON

**Biography / role:** Protagonist, Ella Mae’s older son, and Keith’s older brother. Manny is four years older than Keith and turns sixteen at the Season One finale.  
**Physical description:** No approved detailed physical profile is established in the completed production documents.  
**Personality:** Observant, guarded, disciplined, protective, introspective, and socially alert.  
**Motivation:** Protect Keith, honor Ella Mae’s trust, earn respect without needless escalation, and build a life through basketball and music craft.  
**Internal conflict:** The burden of responsibility versus the limits of what protection can prevent.  
**External conflict:** Neighborhood tests, money/risk proximity, pressure around the home, and the finale’s consequence.  
**Relationships:** Brother to Keith; son of Ella Mae; friend/partner to Butch; creative collaborator with Lucky; respectful non-romantic connection with Sarah; robbery-witness connection with Nate.  
**Season One arc:** Childhood protector → developing DJ/producer and player → trusted party host unable to undo the final event.  
**Future potential:** **REQUIRES ALFRED HJR APPROVAL.** A New York path is not established.

### Keith — CONFIRMED CANON

**Biography / role:** Manny’s younger brother; charismatic social magnet and natural freestyler.  
**Physical description:** No approved detailed physical profile is established in the completed production documents.  
**Personality:** Funny, connective, crowd-seeking, spontaneous, and gifted with voice.  
**Motivation:** Belong, make people laugh, be heard, and participate in the energy around him.  
**Internal conflict:** His appetite for the crowd stands against Manny’s effort to preserve his youth and steer him from risk.  
**External conflict:** Chucky experiences Keith’s invitation and later inattention as rejection.  
**Relationships:** Manny’s brother; creative presence in Lucky’s studio; friend to Chucky; part of the Butch/Lucky/Nate/Sarah circle.  
**Season One arc:** Steering-wheel rhyme → practice-tape discovery → studio and party freestyles → remorse after the confrontation.  
**Future potential:** **REQUIRES ALFRED HJR APPROVAL.** No post-finale music, school, legal, or emotional story is established.

### Ella Mae — CONFIRMED CANON

**Biography / role:** Working mother of Manny and Keith and guardian of the family’s home boundary.  
**Physical description:** No approved detailed physical profile is established in the completed production documents.  
**Personality / motivation:** Practical, protective, trusting where trust is earned, and committed to giving her sons more space and safety.  
**Conflict:** Work shifts can leave the brothers with borrowed freedom; material progress cannot fully remove the neighborhood’s consequences.  
**Relationships:** Mother to Manny and Keith; trusts Uncle Candyman to chaperone the Sweet Sixteen.

## 6. Season-by-Season Overview

### Season One / Book One — CONFIRMED CANON

Season One begins in the Woods: Manny’s early responsibility for Keith, Ella Mae’s protected-home rule, Kizzy, play, fear, and neighborhood learning. Music begins as lived instinct in the junkyard and becomes a craft through Steve’s turntables and Lucky’s studio. The move to the Rocks creates a house, a court, and new tests rather than a complete escape.

Manny meets Butch through basketball; Nate enters through the aftermath of Manny’s coat robbery and a Coney reunion; Sarah enters at high school and meets Butch first. Friendship, music, money, and belonging build toward Manny’s Sweet Sixteen. The party begins as music and community, then moves through cake, cleanup, alcohol, weed comedy, a final dance, Chucky’s growing exclusion, rain, a front-yard fight, a record scratch, and a 911 call. The season ends with Chucky unresponsive and Keith unable to undo what happened.

## 8. Production Style

### Cinematography and camera language — CONFIRMED DIRECTION

Dark urban realism with gothic sensibilities: silhouettes, gates, moonlight, urban architecture, selective highlights, and an emphasis on faces, hands, records, courts, porches, fences, and thresholds. Darkness should support emotional weight, never obscure story information.

### Editing

**CONFIRMED DIRECTION:** Let music, rhythm, neighborhood observation, and emotional reaction carry transitions. Build the party arc with geography and clock progression rather than disorienting montage.  
**REQUIRES ALFRED HJR APPROVAL:** exact editorial grammar, camera format, aspect ratio, and pacing targets.

### Music and sound design — CONFIRMED DIRECTION

Treat records, turntables, freestyles, DJ selections, speaker energy, silence, rain, and the record scratch as story action. Music is not wallpaper. The final scratch is a narrative event.

### Color palette — CONFIRMED PRODUCTION GUIDANCE

Primary visual palette: deep black, metallic silver, midnight blue, and gun-metal gray. The visual atmosphere is moody, consequential, and street-level; fashion and styling remain authentic rather than caricatured.

### Opening titles and episode endings

**CONFIRMED DIRECTION:** Use the established visual world—urban architecture, silhouettes, gates, selective highlights, records, courts, and thresholds—as source-aligned title imagery. Season One endings preserve the emotional cost of the episode; the finale ends unresolved.  
**REQUIRES ALFRED HJR APPROVAL:** a final main-title design, theme song, title-card sequence, and a mandatory formula for future episode endings.
